Secondary Glazing for Heritage Buildings — What You Need to Know

If you own a listed building or live in a conservation area, you've probably hit the same wall as most people: you want to improve your windows, but you're not sure what you're allowed to do. The answer is often simpler than you think — and secondary glazing is usually where the conversation starts.

We introduced our secondary glazing solution for exactly this situation. It's designed for sash windows and period properties where you want meaningful improvement without touching the original fabric of the building. Here's everything you actually need to know about it.

What secondary glazing is — and what it isn't

Secondary glazing is a separate glazed panel fitted to the room-side of your existing window. It sits behind the original sash, leaving the outside completely unchanged. From the street, your windows look exactly the same. From inside, you gain an additional layer of insulation.

This is important to be clear about: secondary glazing is not double glazing in the conventional sense. You're not replacing the original glass or the original frames. You're adding a discreet inner panel that does the thermal and acoustic work without altering the window itself.

That distinction is why it doesn't require listed building consent. The original window stays intact. No planning application needed.

Why ours is different

There's a lot of secondary glazing on the market, and most of it looks like secondary glazing. White plastic frames, visible fixings, panels that rattle when you open the inner sash. It's immediately obvious what it is.

Ours is built to a different specification. The frames are timber — painted to match your existing joinery, or stained if that's what your windows require. There is no white plastic, no aluminium. When it's fitted properly, it blends into the room. From a distance, most people don't notice it's there.

The panels are fixed using advanced magnetic tape. This means:

  • The panels can be removed easily for cleaning — the glass on both sides stays accessible
  • The seal is firm and consistent, not reliant on clips or catches that loosen over time
  • Fitting is precise and the panel sits flush against the original window

Critically, our secondary glazing is designed so the original sash windows remain fully operational. You can still open and close them normally. The secondary panel comes away cleanly when you need it to and goes back just as easily. This isn't always the case with secondary glazing — many systems make the original window difficult or impossible to use once fitted.

What it actually does

The difference you notice immediately is draught. Sash windows — particularly original Victorian and Georgian ones — are rarely properly sealed, and the gaps let cold air in all winter. Secondary glazing eliminates this almost entirely.

The thermal improvement is significant. A well-fitted secondary glazing system can reduce heat loss through windows by up to 70%. For a period property with large sash windows, that's a meaningful change in comfort and running costs.

Acoustic improvement is the other benefit people underestimate. Secondary glazing with the right air gap is more effective at reducing external noise than conventional double glazing — because the wider gap between the two panes absorbs more sound energy. If you're on a busy Edinburgh street, the difference is noticeable within the first night.

We use high-performance plexiglass — not standard glass — in our secondary panels. It's lighter, which means less stress on the magnetic fixings, and it has slightly better thermal properties than standard float glass at the same thickness.

Who it's right for

Secondary glazing makes sense in a few specific situations:

  • Listed buildings where replacing or upgrading the original windows requires consent and you want a simpler route
  • Conservation area properties where your permitted development rights are restricted
  • Buildings where the original windows are in good condition and replacement would be a waste — secondary glazing is the eco-friendly choice when the frames are sound
  • Commercial properties where disruption needs to be minimal — we fitted secondary glazing throughout Fraser Suites Edinburgh (156 windows) without scaffolding and without interrupting hotel operations
  • Anyone wanting a fast improvement — secondary glazing doesn't require planning, so the lead time is significantly shorter than a full window replacement or upgrade project

Paired with draught proofing

Most of the sash windows we see in Edinburgh have never been draught proofed. The original frames are in reasonable condition, but the gap between the sashes — and between the sash and the frame — means the window leaks air freely. Secondary glazing helps. Draught proofing first makes the difference even more dramatic.

We typically recommend treating both together. Draught proofing involves fitting precision-milled brush pile seals into the frame, which stops air movement around the sash. Combined with secondary glazing, the result is a window that performs close to modern double glazing standards — while looking completely original from outside.
