Top 10 Planning Tips for Listed Property Owners

What you need to know before you touch a window, door, or any original feature in a Scottish listed building. Written from real experience.

From the team

Hard-earned lessons from working on listed properties across Scotland

We have worked on hundreds of listed buildings in Edinburgh and beyond — from single residential properties to Category A commercial buildings with over 150 windows. Along the way we've seen every planning pitfall, every frustrating delay, and every avoidable mistake that owners make when they don't know the rules.

This is a straight-talking guide to the 10 things listed property owners most need to know before starting any window or door work. Read it before you do anything else.

The list

10 tips and gotchas you need to know

01

Listed building consent is not the same as planning permission — and you need both.

Most owners assume listed building consent and planning permission are the same thing. They are not. Planning permission controls use and development. Listed building consent controls alterations to the character of a listed building. Window replacement on a listed building requires listed building consent, and in some cases also requires standard planning permission if the character of the building is significantly changed. Apply for the right consents, in the right order.

02

C-listed properties need consent too — don't assume you're exempt.

A-listed and B-listed buildings are well understood. C-listed is where owners most often get caught out. In Scotland, C-listed properties are those of local importance. They still require listed building consent for alterations to their character. If your property is C-listed and you replace windows without consent, you are committing an offence. Contact your local planning authority before starting any work.

03

Conservation area rules apply even if your building is not listed.

Being in a conservation area — without being listed — still places restrictions on your windows and doors. In Edinburgh's conservation areas (New Town, Old Town, Southside and others), Article 4 Directions remove permitted development rights, meaning work you could do freely on an unlisted property requires formal consent. Check your property's conservation area designation before assuming you can act without approval.

04

Work done without consent creates problems when you sell.

Unauthorised works to a listed building or conservation area property don't disappear. They show up in property searches. Solicitors will raise them. Buyers' lenders may refuse to lend. You may be required to reinstate the original features at your own cost before a sale can complete. The "it'll never be noticed" gamble is rarely worth it.

05

The glazing must be on the council's approved list — not just "heritage-style".

Lots of window companies market products as "heritage glazing" or "slim-line double glazing for listed buildings." That does not mean those units are on Edinburgh City Council's approved list. Only specific products — tested, documented, and pre-approved — are permitted in listed and conservation area buildings in Edinburgh. Always confirm the exact specification is on the approved list before committing. We only use pre-approved units as a matter of course.

06

Secondary glazing is your consent-free option — and it's better than you think.

Secondary glazing — a removable inner panel fitted to the room-side of the existing window — does not require listed building consent. It leaves the original windows entirely intact. Our timber-framed secondary glazing is painted to match, invisible from outside, and dramatically reduces heat loss and noise. If you need a fast solution or cannot face the consent process, secondary glazing is a serious option — not a compromise.

07

Use a contractor who handles the consent process — don't do it yourself.

Listed building consent applications require technical drawings, materials specifications, and a clear statement of why the works are appropriate. Applications submitted without this supporting information are routinely rejected, causing months of delay. An experienced contractor — one who knows what conservation officers expect — will submit an application that moves through the system. We handle this on behalf of every client, using materials and specifications that are pre-agreed with Edinburgh City Council.

08

Repair is always considered before replacement — plan accordingly.

Conservation officers have a clear hierarchy: repair first, replace only if repair is not viable. If you go into a consent application asking to replace windows that could be repaired, you will likely be refused. A good survey and honest condition report is essential. If windows can be repaired to a good standard, that is the right answer — both for consent purposes and for your building's long-term character.

09

Lead times on the consent process — build this into your project timeline.

Listed building consent applications in Edinburgh typically take 8–12 weeks from submission to decision, assuming the application is complete and well-prepared. Add the time to prepare the application (2–4 weeks) and then manufacturing lead time for the windows themselves (8–10 weeks). A project you want to start in spring may need to have its consent process started the previous autumn. Plan early.

10

A pre-application conversation with the planning department costs nothing and saves a lot.

Before submitting any formal application, you can request a pre-application discussion with Edinburgh City Council's planning and conservation team. This is free and informal. It allows you to float your proposal, get initial feedback, and understand what is likely to be acceptable before committing to a full application. Most experienced contractors do this as a matter of course. It can save you months and considerable expense if you discover early that your initial approach is unlikely to be supported.
